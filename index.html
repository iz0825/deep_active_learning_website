Elisa Chotzoglou is currently with the Department of Computing at Imperial College London. She obtained her B.Sc and M.Sc from Department of Computer Science and Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering departments respectively. She has been research assistant on multimodal deep learning and deep learning for genomics. Her research interests include machine learning, medical image analysis, computer vision and anomaly detection.

Past Research

Elisa's past research was mainly oriented around:

  • Research on multimodal Deep Learning. Deep Learning architectures such as Restricted Boltzmann machines and autoencoders, Convolutional Neural Networks and Recurrent Neural networks (LSTMs) are used to learn a common latent space from multimodal data. Implementation of a multimodal retrieval system for text, image, speech using the above methods.
  • Deep Learning for Genomics. Use convolutional autoencoders and LSTMs for detection, clustering and classification of patterns from nucleotides sequences in cells. Implementation using tensorflow framework.

But what is deep learning?

Nowadays, machine learning technology has been integrated into our daily lives, from content filtering, personalised recommendations on social networks to image recognition. Often, such technologies need to be able to identify main components of images, select relevant data and information, translate speech into text and so on. Instead of the conventional machine learning algorithms that require large amount of human efforts and specific information related to each activity, deep learning is a form of representation learning that allows the machines to take in raw data input and transform it into more abstract representation, in order to help future processes. The main idea behind this is to transform the data - for example, an image - one step at a time until reaching the highest abstract level. The most promising advantage deep learning methods bring is that the algorithm will be able to use a general-purpose learning algorithm to train itself to process the data. [1]
